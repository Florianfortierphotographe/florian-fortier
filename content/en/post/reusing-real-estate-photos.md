When a property comes back on the market, a common question arises among real estate agents, particularly in competitive areas like Paris and the Île-de-France region: **can you reuse the photos taken during the previous mandate?**

The images already exist. They have been paid for. They seem perfectly usable. However, from both a legal and strategic standpoint, the answer isn’t as straightforward as it seems. It all depends on the context, and most importantly, whether the property is staying with the same agency or switching to a new one.

## Who Really Owns Real Estate Photos?

In France, a photograph is protected by copyright law. The photographer retains ownership of the rights unless a specific, written transfer of rights is agreed upon. 

In the context of a real estate mandate, the agency does not become the owner of the images. Instead, they are granted a *license to use* them, defined by a contract. This right of use can be limited in time, geographical scope, or the type of media. In fast-moving markets like Paris, understanding this distinction is crucial. **A photograph is not just a digital file; it is a legally protected creative work.**

## If the Property Stays with the Same Agency

When a property is relisted by the same agency, the situation seems simpler, but it still requires a quick contract check.

If the licensing rights granted during the first mandate only covered the initial marketing period, republishing the listing might require a license renewal. Some agreements explicitly include the possibility of reuse for a relisting, while others do not. For a professional approach, it is always best to clarify this before republishing to protect both the agency and the photographer.

## If the Property Changes Agencies

The situation shifts entirely when a property leaves one agency for another. In this case, **the new agency cannot freely use the old photographs.**

The initial contract linked the photographer specifically to the first agency. The rights of use were granted to that particular business, not to the property owner or a third party. Even if the seller possesses the digital files, they do not have the legal authority to grant a new commercial license. Using these images without the photographer's consent constitutes copyright infringement and can seriously damage the new agency's professional credibility.

## Can the Owner Decide Alone?

Some sellers assume they own the images because they indirectly financed the photoshoot through the agency’s fees. Legally, this is incorrect. Paying for a service does not automatically transfer copyright. Without a clear, signed agreement, any new commercial use must be validated by the creator of the images.

## A Strategic Decision, Not Just a Legal One

Beyond the legal framework, there is a strong marketing aspect to consider. A property coming back on the market often deserves a fresh visual approach. In highly competitive markets, keeping the exact same images can make the listing look stagnant or give buyers the impression that the property is struggling to sell. Even when reuse is legally permitted, it isn’t always the best commercial strategy.

## The Risks of Unauthorized Use

Publishing images without proper authorization can lead to the immediate removal of the listing visuals, financial compensation demands, or formal litigation. In the high-end real estate sector, reputational risk is often far more damaging than the cost of simply commissioning a new photoshoot.

## Clarify to Secure Your Business

For real estate professionals, transparency is the best policy. Clarifying the duration of image rights and anticipating what happens if the property changes hands prevents any gray areas. High-quality imagery is a direct reflection of your brand's positioning.

## Conclusion

Can you reuse photos from an old real estate mandate? **It depends entirely on the context.** If the property remains with the same agency and the licensing agreement allows it, it is possible. If the property changes agencies, new authorization is absolutely mandatory.

However, keep in mind that relisting a property is often the perfect opportunity to rethink its visual presentation and attract a new pool of buyers.
